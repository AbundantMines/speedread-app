How the Reading Speed Calculator Works

This calculator divides the number of words you read by the time it took you, giving your reading speed in words per minute (WPM). WPM is the universal measure of reading speed used by educators, researchers, and speed reading programs.

For the most accurate result, time yourself reading a full article or book chapter — not just a paragraph. Longer samples reduce the impact of start/stop delays.

What's a Good Reading Speed?

Reading speeds vary by age, material difficulty, and practice. Here's how adults typically break down:

  • Below 150 WPM: Below average — may indicate reading difficulties or very dense material
  • 200–250 WPM: Average adult reading speed (the U.S. national average is 238 WPM)
  • 300–400 WPM: Above average — typical of college-educated readers and avid readers
  • 400–700 WPM: Speed reader territory — achievable with RSVP training and practice
  • 700+ WPM: Elite — top 1% of readers, usually with extensive speed reading experience

How to Improve Your Reading Speed

  1. Eliminate subvocalization — Stop "saying" words in your head. This caps most readers at 250 WPM.
  2. Reduce eye fixations — Train your eyes to take in groups of words instead of individual words.
  3. Use RSVP — Rapid Serial Visual Presentation removes all eye movement, letting your brain focus purely on comprehension. Learn more about RSVP →
  4. Practice daily — Even 10 minutes/day builds speed. WarpReader's 28-day training course is designed for this.
  5. Track your progress — What gets measured gets improved. Use the free speed test weekly to benchmark.

Reading Speed by Context

Your WPM will vary by what you're reading:

  • Technical/legal documents: 150–200 WPM (dense, requires re-reading)
  • Non-fiction books: 200–300 WPM (moderate density)
  • Fiction/narrative: 250–350 WPM (flowing, less re-reading)
  • Email/social media: 300–400 WPM (familiar formats, skim-friendly)
