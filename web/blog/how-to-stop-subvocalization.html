How to Stop Subvocalization: The #1 Speed Reading Bottleneck

Updated April 23, 2026 · 11 min read

Right now, as you read these words, there's a voice in your head pronouncing each one. You can hear it. It's your voice, at roughly the speed you'd speak aloud.

That voice is called subvocalization, and it's the single biggest reason most adults read at 200–300 words per minute — the same speed they read at in middle school.

Your visual cortex can process text at 700–1,000+ WPM. Your inner voice tops out at 300 WPM. The voice is the bottleneck. Here's how to loosen it.

What Is Subvocalization?

Subvocalization is the internal speech that accompanies reading. When you read, your brain activates the same motor cortex regions used for speaking — micro-movements in the larynx, tongue, and lips. You're silently "saying" every word, even though no sound comes out.

This isn't a bad habit you developed. It's how everyone learns to read. Children start by reading aloud, then transition to whispering, then to silent reading with internal speech. For most people, that's where the progression stops.

But there's a further stage: visual processing without internal speech. This is how speed readers process text — seeing words and extracting meaning directly, the same way you process a stop sign or a familiar logo without "saying" it in your head.

Why It's the Biggest Speed Bottleneck

Consider the math:

  • Average speaking speed: 125–175 WPM
  • Average subvocalization speed: 200–300 WPM (slightly faster than speaking because there's no physical articulation)
  • Visual processing speed: 700–1,000+ WPM

The gap: Your inner voice is 3–5× slower than your visual system. Every word that passes through the auditory bottleneck is a word that could have been processed visually at triple the speed.

Subvocalization doesn't just cap your speed — it compounds other problems:

  • It enables mind-wandering. Because reading at 250 WPM leaves so much unused brain capacity, your mind fills the gap with unrelated thoughts. Read more about why you can't focus while reading.
  • It makes reading feel effortful. Maintaining the inner voice requires constant low-level muscular activation. Over long sessions, this creates fatigue.
  • It limits comprehension at high speeds. If you try to speed up while still subvocalizing, comprehension drops — not because you're going too fast for your brain, but because you're going too fast for your inner voice, and the incomplete subvocalization creates interference.

Should You Eliminate It Completely?

No. And you probably can't — nor should you try.

Some level of subvocalization is natural and even helpful for complex or emotionally resonant text. Poetry, philosophical arguments, and beautifully crafted prose benefit from the "inner voice" because the auditory quality is part of the experience.

The goal isn't zero subvocalization. It's flexible subvocalization — the ability to dial it up or down based on the material:

  • Full subvocalization: Poetry, legal documents, dialogue-heavy fiction — savor the words
  • Partial subvocalization: General nonfiction, news, business reports — get the meaning efficiently
  • Minimal subvocalization: Familiar topics, scanning for information, high-speed reading — pure visual processing

Speed readers don't eliminate the inner voice. They learn to control it.

5 Proven Techniques to Reduce Subvocalization

1 RSVP at 400+ WPM

This is the most effective method by far. RSVP (Rapid Serial Visual Presentation) displays words one at a time at a fixed point. When you set the speed above 400 WPM, your brain literally cannot subvocalize fast enough to keep up.

What happens: for the first few minutes, you feel like you're missing words. Then something clicks — your brain switches from auditory processing to visual processing. You stop "hearing" the words and start "seeing" the meaning. This is the breakthrough moment.

Try it: Open WarpReader, paste a familiar article, set the speed to 450 WPM, and read for 5 minutes. Don't worry about comprehension initially. Just let your brain adapt to the speed.

2 Hum or Count While Reading

Occupy your inner voice with a competing task. Hum a steady tone, count "1-2-3-4" repeatedly, or say "A-E-I-O-U" while reading. This hijacks the auditory processing channel and forces your brain to process text visually.

This feels incredibly awkward for the first few minutes. That's normal. Comprehension drops initially, then recovers as your visual processing takes over. Practice 5 minutes per day for one week.

3 Chew Gum

Subvocalization involves micro-movements of the lips, tongue, and larynx. Chewing gum disrupts these movements, reducing the physical component of inner speech.

It's a mild intervention — won't transform your reading speed alone — but stacks well with other techniques. Think of it as training wheels for subvocalization reduction.

4 Press Your Tongue to the Roof of Your Mouth

Similar to gum chewing but more deliberate. Pressing your tongue firmly against your hard palate makes it physically difficult to form the micro-articulations that accompany subvocalization.

Combined with faster-than-comfortable reading (whether RSVP or traditional), this can reduce subvocalization by 30–50% within a single session.

5 Use Finger Pacing at Uncomfortable Speeds

For traditional (non-RSVP) reading, move your finger or a pen under the text at a pace faster than your inner voice can follow. Your eyes will track the pacer, and your brain will start processing words visually to keep up.

Start by pacing at your normal speed, then gradually accelerate over 5 minutes until comprehension begins to drop. Hold at that edge for a few minutes. When you slow back down, your new "comfortable" speed will be 20–30% faster.

Realistic Timeline for Reducing Subvocalization

Week 1: Awareness

You become conscious of your inner voice while reading. This awareness alone changes your reading. Start RSVP practice at 400 WPM for 10 minutes daily. Try the humming technique for 5 minutes per session.

Expected result: You can feel the difference between subvocalized and visual processing. Speed gains are inconsistent.

Week 2: Breakthrough

Your brain starts defaulting to visual processing during RSVP sessions. Subvocalization becomes "optional" rather than automatic at high speeds.

Expected result: Comfortable RSVP reading at 450–500 WPM with 70%+ comprehension. Traditional reading speed up 50–80 WPM.

Weeks 3–4: Consolidation

Visual processing becomes your default for familiar content. You still subvocalize for complex or novel material, but you can consciously switch between modes.

Expected result: Comfortable RSVP at 500–700 WPM. Traditional reading speed up 100–150 WPM from baseline. You've gained flexible control.

Common Myths About Subvocalization

Myth: "Eliminating subvocalization destroys comprehension"

Partially true at first — comprehension dips during the transition period. But it recovers within days as visual processing strengthens. Long-term, speed readers with reduced subvocalization maintain 80%+ comprehension at 2–3× normal speeds. The research on this is consistent. See our overview of speed reading myths debunked.

Myth: "You need subvocalization to understand text"

You don't subvocalize when reading road signs, brand logos, or emoticons. You process them visually and extract meaning instantly. Words can work the same way — especially common words and phrases that you've seen thousands of times.

Myth: "Only gifted people can read without subvocalizing"

Subvocalization reduction is a trainable skill. It's not talent — it's practice. Most people can significantly reduce subvocalization within 2 weeks of daily RSVP practice. Measure your starting speed, practice for two weeks, and retest.

Myth: "Speed reading means you lose the 'joy' of reading"

Remember: the goal is flexible control, not permanent elimination. You can still savor prose when you want to. But for the 80% of reading that's informational — emails, articles, reports, textbooks — reduced subvocalization saves hours per week.
