How fast should you read? It depends on your age, but probably faster than you think. Most adults read at the same speed they reached in middle school — not because they can't improve, but because nobody ever tells them to.

Here's the data on average reading speeds by age group, what affects those numbers, and how to move from average to exceptional.

Why Reading Speed Plateaus After Childhood

Children's reading speed increases because they're actively learning to read. Their brains are building neural pathways for word recognition, developing sight vocabulary, and transitioning from phonetic decoding to whole-word recognition.

Around age 12–14, this learning stops — not because the brain is "done" learning, but because the educational system stops teaching reading mechanics. After middle school, reading instruction focuses on comprehension, analysis, and interpretation. Speed is never addressed again.

The result: most adults are stuck with the reading mechanics they developed at age 13. The brain is perfectly capable of reading at 400, 500, or 700+ WPM — it just hasn't been trained to do so.

Key insight: Reading speed is a skill, not a fixed trait. It plateaus because training stops, not because improvement stops being possible. Adults who practice speed reading techniques typically see 60–100% improvement within 30 days.

Factors That Affect Reading Speed

Text difficulty

Everyone reads familiar, simple text faster than dense, technical material. A novel at 300 WPM, a physics textbook at 150 WPM. This is normal and appropriate — you should adjust speed to content complexity.

Vocabulary

Words you've seen before are processed faster (100–200ms) than unfamiliar words (300–500ms). This is why voracious readers tend to be faster readers — their sight vocabulary is larger.

Interest and motivation

You read faster when you're engaged. Boring material triggers more mind-wandering and regressions, both of which reduce effective speed. See why you can't focus while reading.

Medium

Screen vs. paper affects speed by 10–20% for most people, depending on the screen type, text formatting, and lighting. Well-formatted digital text on a good screen approaches paper-reading speeds.

Vision

Uncorrected vision problems (common in adults who haven't had a recent eye exam) can reduce reading speed by 15–30%. If you're reading slower than expected, get your eyes checked first.

Subvocalization habits

Heavy subvocalizers (who "hear" every word) top out at 250–300 WPM. Light subvocalizers (who hear only key words) can reach 400–500 WPM with traditional reading. And people who've trained to reduce subvocalization can hit 600+ WPM. See our guide on how to stop subvocalization.

Children's Reading Speed (Ages 6–12)

Children's reading development follows a predictable progression:

Ages 6–7 (Learning to decode): Reading is effortful. Children sound out words phonetically. Speed is 30–80 WPM. Comprehension is limited to simple sentences.

Ages 7–9 (Building fluency): Sight word vocabulary grows rapidly. Children recognize common words instantly and only need to decode unfamiliar ones. Speed reaches 90–150 WPM.

Ages 9–11 (Reading to learn): The critical transition. Reading shifts from a skill being learned to a tool for learning other subjects. Subvocalization becomes the primary reading mode. Speed reaches 120–200 WPM.

Ages 11–13 (Plateau zone): Adult reading patterns establish. Speed reaches 150–250 WPM and then stops increasing without intervention.

If your child is below benchmark for their age, the most helpful intervention is more reading practice — not speed techniques. Speed reading methods are most effective after age 12, once basic reading fluency is established.

Teens and College Students (Ages 13–22)

This is the ideal age to learn speed reading techniques. The brain is still highly plastic, reading skills are established but not fossilized, and the academic workload provides both motivation and practice material.

College students who learn RSVP-based speed reading typically see the largest and fastest improvements:

  • Baseline: 250–300 WPM average
  • After 2 weeks of practice: 400–500 WPM
  • After 30 days: 500–700 WPM

The academic payoff is enormous. A student reading at 600 WPM instead of 250 WPM can read the same textbook chapters in 40% of the time — freeing hours per week for other work (or, realistically, sleep). See our guide on speed reading for students.

Adults (Ages 23–65+)

Adult reading speed averages 238 WPM across studies, with relatively little variation by age within this range. The slight decline after age 65 is associated with vision changes and processing speed, not cognitive ability.

The good news: adults improve just as well as younger readers when they practice. The brain's ability to learn faster reading patterns doesn't disappear with age — it just needs to be activated.

Adults often see slightly slower initial progress than teens (2–3 weeks to significant improvement vs. 1–2 weeks), but reach the same endpoints. A 50-year-old who practices RSVP daily for 30 days can reach 500+ WPM just as reliably as a 20-year-old.
