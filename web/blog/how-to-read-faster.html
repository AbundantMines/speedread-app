How to Read Faster: 7 Proven Speed Reading Techniques That Actually Work

Updated April 7, 2026 · 11 min read

The 7 Techniques

  • 1. RSVP (Rapid Serial Visual Presentation)
  • 2. Reduce Subvocalization
  • 3. Use a Visual Pacer
  • 4. Expand Peripheral Vision
  • 5. Stop Regressing
  • 6. Preview Before Deep Reading
  • 7. Train with Increasing Speeds
  • Your 30-Day Speed Reading Plan

The average adult reads at 238 words per minute. That speed was set somewhere between third and fifth grade, and for most people, it never changes. Not because it can't — but because nobody ever teaches you how.

The truth is, your brain can process text at 2–3× the speed you're reading right now. The bottleneck isn't intelligence or comprehension — it's a set of mechanical habits that slow you down without you realizing it.

Here are seven techniques that actually work. They're ordered from most effective to supplementary, based on the research and the results we've seen from thousands of WarpReader users.

First, though: take the free speed test to get your baseline. You'll need it to measure your progress.

1 RSVP — Rapid Serial Visual Presentation

Impact: ★★★★★ | Difficulty: Easy | Time to see results: Day 1

RSVP is the single most effective speed reading technique available. Instead of your eyes scanning across lines of text, words appear one at a time at a fixed point on screen. Your eyes never move.

Why it's so effective:

  • Eliminates saccadic eye movement — those 3–5 jumps your eyes make per line eat up 20–30% of reading time
  • Kills regressions — you can't re-read a word that's already been replaced
  • Forces visual processing — at 400+ WPM, your brain switches from subvocalization to direct visual recognition

Most people see an immediate 30–50% speed improvement on their first RSVP session. Within 30 days of 10-minute daily practice, 2–3× your baseline is typical.

Try it now: Open WarpReader, paste any article, and start reading. Set your speed to your current WPM, then bump it up 50 after 5 minutes. Learn more about how RSVP works.

2 Reduce Subvocalization

Impact: ★★★★☆ | Difficulty: Moderate | Time to see results: 1–2 weeks

Subvocalization is the "inner voice" that pronounces each word as you read. It's how you learned to read as a child — sounding out words one at a time. For most adults, this voice never goes away.

The problem: your inner voice speaks at 200–300 WPM. Your visual cortex can process language at 1,000+ WPM. That inner voice is the ceiling.

How to reduce it:

  1. Hum or count while reading. This occupies your auditory system and forces visual processing. It feels bizarre at first. Do it for 5 minutes a day.
  2. Read faster than you can subvocalize. RSVP at 400+ WPM naturally forces this transition — your brain can't pronounce words that fast, so it stops trying.
  3. Chew gum. Seriously. It disrupts the micro-movements in your larynx that accompany subvocalization.
  4. Focus on meaning, not words. Instead of "hearing" individual words, try to absorb the meaning of phrases and sentences as visual patterns.

You don't need to eliminate subvocalization completely. Reducing it by 50% typically adds 100–150 WPM to your reading speed.

3 Use a Visual Pacer

Impact: ★★★☆☆ | Difficulty: Easy | Time to see results: Immediate

A visual pacer — your finger, a pen, or a cursor — moving along the line of text gives your eyes a physical target to follow. It sounds too simple to work. It does work.

Research shows that a visual pacer reduces the number of fixations per line and virtually eliminates regressions. Your eyes follow the moving target instead of wandering.

How to use it effectively:

  • Move your pacer at a steady speed, slightly faster than comfortable
  • Don't follow every word — let the pacer lead and your eyes follow roughly
  • Increase the pacer speed by 10% every few pages
  • For digital reading, RSVP is essentially the ultimate pacer — the word itself is the moving target

4 Expand Peripheral Vision

Impact: ★★★☆☆ | Difficulty: Moderate | Time to see results: 2–4 weeks

Your foveal vision (the sharp center of your visual field) covers about 2 degrees. But your peripheral vision can detect shapes and patterns much wider. Training peripheral vision lets you process more words per fixation.

Practical exercises:

  1. The column technique: Focus on the center of a newspaper column (or any narrow text). Try to read each line with a single fixation at the center, using peripheral vision for the edges.
  2. Schulte tables: Grid exercises where you find numbers in sequence while keeping your eyes fixed at the center. Five minutes a day builds peripheral word recognition.
  3. Chunking practice: Instead of reading word by word, practice absorbing 3–4 word phrases in a single fixation. Start with simple text and gradually move to more complex material.

This technique compounds well with RSVP. Once you've expanded your peripheral processing, some RSVP apps (including WarpReader) can show 2–3 words at a time instead of one.

5 Stop Regressing

Impact: ★★★★☆ | Difficulty: Easy | Time to see results: Immediate

Regressions are backward eye movements — re-reading words or phrases you already passed. Studies show the average reader regresses 10–15% of the time, often unconsciously.

Most regressions aren't because you didn't understand something. They're a habit. Your eyes jump back reflexively, not purposefully.

How to stop:

  • Use a physical cover. Place a card or your hand above the line you're reading, covering text you've already read. This physically prevents regression.
  • Use RSVP. When words replace each other on screen, regression is literally impossible. This is one of RSVP's biggest structural advantages.
  • Trust your first pass. Most "missed" comprehension comes back from context within 1–2 sentences. You rarely need to re-read.

Eliminating regressions alone typically adds 10–15% to your reading speed — with zero additional effort.

6 Preview Before Deep Reading

Impact: ★★★☆☆ | Difficulty: Easy | Time to see results: Immediate

Before reading a book chapter, article, or report, spend 2 minutes scanning:

  • All headings and subheadings
  • The first sentence of each paragraph
  • Any bolded text, lists, or call-out boxes
  • The conclusion or summary

This creates a mental scaffold — a framework that your brain fills in during the actual read. Research from cognitive psychology consistently shows that activated prior knowledge accelerates processing speed by 20–30%.

Think of it like watching a movie trailer before the film. You already know the general arc, so you can focus on the details.

7 Train with Increasing Speeds

Impact: ★★★★☆ | Difficulty: Easy | Time to see results: 1 week

This is the "interval training" of speed reading. Just like athletes alternate between high-intensity and recovery, speed readers alternate between challenging speeds and comfortable ones.

The 5-Minute Speed Drill

  1. Minute 1: Read at your comfortable speed (warm-up)
  2. Minute 2: Increase by 100 WPM (challenging but readable)
  3. Minute 3: Increase by another 100 WPM (you'll lose some comprehension — that's fine)
  4. Minute 4: Drop to +50 WPM above baseline (this suddenly feels easy)
  5. Minute 5: Read any content at this new "easy" speed

Why it works: The overshoot in minute 3 recalibrates your brain's sense of "fast." When you drop back down, your new normal is 50–100 WPM faster than where you started.

Do this drill once a day for 7 days. Most people gain 50–100 WPM permanently.

Your 30-Day Speed Reading Plan

Here's how to combine all seven techniques into a practical training plan:

Week 1: Foundation

  • Take the speed test — establish your baseline
  • Read 15 minutes daily on WarpReader at your baseline speed
  • Focus on getting comfortable with RSVP (technique #1)
  • Preview content before reading (technique #6)

Week 2: Push

  • Start the 5-minute speed drill daily (technique #7)
  • Practice subvocalization reduction for 5 minutes (technique #2)
  • Increase RSVP speed by 50 WPM above your new comfort level

Week 3: Consolidate

  • Continue daily drills at progressively higher speeds
  • Add peripheral vision exercises — 5 minutes daily (technique #4)
  • Practice with varied content: articles, books, reports

Week 4: Master

  • Retest your speed — expect 60–100% improvement
  • Read daily content at your new speed
  • Push to 2× baseline in RSVP sessions
  • The speed is now automatic — maintenance is 10 minutes/day
