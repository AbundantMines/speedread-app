"How fast should I read?" It's a simple question with a nuanced answer. The right reading speed depends on what you're reading, why you're reading it, and what you plan to do with the information.

But if you want benchmarks — concrete WPM targets to aim for — here they are.

Speed Depends on Context

A single WPM number is misleading. Good readers don't read everything at the same speed — they adjust based on content:

Content you should read slower

  • Legal documents: Every word matters. 200–300 WPM.
  • Dense technical material: New concepts require processing time. 250–400 WPM.
  • Poetry and literary prose: The rhythm and sound matter. Your natural pace.
  • Instructions / recipes / code: You're reading to execute, not absorb. Slow and precise.

Content you should read faster

  • News articles: Low density, familiar patterns. 500–800 WPM.
  • Email and messages: Fast scan for action items. 400–600 WPM.
  • Business books (after chapter 1): Most ideas are repeated 3× with different examples. 500–700 WPM.
  • Familiar topics: If you already know the subject, speed through for new insights. 600+ WPM.
  • Re-reads: You've already processed it once. Push to max speed.

The goal isn't a single high WPM number. It's a wide range — being able to read at 250 WPM for a contract and 700 WPM for a news article, and knowing when to use which speed.

Effective WPM vs Raw WPM

Raw WPM measures how fast words pass through your visual field. Effective WPM measures how much information you actually retain.

The formula: Effective WPM = Raw WPM × Comprehension %

Examples:

  • 500 WPM with 80% comprehension = 400 effective WPM ✅
  • 800 WPM with 40% comprehension = 320 effective WPM ❌
  • 300 WPM with 95% comprehension = 285 effective WPM (safe but slow)

The person reading at 500 WPM with good comprehension is processing more information than the person "speed reading" at 800 WPM with poor comprehension. Effective WPM is the only metric that matters.

The sweet spot: For most people, maximum effective WPM occurs at 80–85% comprehension. Pushing for 100% comprehension means reading slower than necessary. Dropping below 70% means speed gains are illusory — you'll need to re-read.

Realistic Targets by Goal

Goal: Read more books

Target: 400–500 WPM with 80% comprehension. At 500 WPM and 30 minutes/day, you'll read 90+ books per year. This is achievable within 30 days of practice for most adults.

Goal: Get through school faster

Target: 450–600 WPM for general textbook reading. For student speed reading, the payoff is enormous: halving your reading time frees 4–8 hours per week.

Goal: Professional development

Target: 400–500 WPM for reports, articles, and industry reading. Professionals who read at this speed can stay current across multiple fields. Use RSVP for PDFs and the Chrome extension for web content.

Goal: Competitive / extreme speed reading

Target: 700–1,000+ WPM. This requires dedicated daily practice over months, near-elimination of subvocalization, and extensive RSVP training. Achievable but not typical. Most people don't need this — 500 WPM solves most practical reading problems.

Goal: Reading with ADHD

Target: 350–500 WPM — but with a focus on sustained comprehension, not raw speed. For ADHD readers, the real win is being able to finish articles and chapters consistently, which RSVP enables through external pacing.

How to Reach Your Target WPM

Regardless of your target, the path is the same:

Step 1: Know your starting point

Take the speed test. You need a real number, not a guess. Most people overestimate their reading speed by 30–50%.

Step 2: Start RSVP training

Open WarpReader. Start at your baseline WPM. Read for 15 minutes. Then push up by 50 WPM. This daily practice is the engine of improvement.

Step 3: Practice the speed drill

The speed interval drill: 1 minute at baseline → 1 minute at +100 → 1 minute at +200 → drop to +50. This recalibrates your brain's sense of "normal" speed. Do this once daily.

Step 4: Work on subvocalization

If you're targeting 400+ WPM, you'll need to reduce subvocalization. RSVP at high speeds does this naturally — your brain can't pronounce words at 500 WPM, so it stops trying.

Step 5: Test weekly

Retest every week. Track both raw WPM and comprehension. Graph your effective WPM over time. Most people see their steepest gains in weeks 1–4.

Typical progression

  • Week 1: Baseline → +30–50% (mostly from eliminating regressions)
  • Week 2: → +50–70% (subvocalization reduction begins)
  • Week 3: → +60–80% (visual processing becoming default)
  • Week 4: → +80–100% (speed stabilizes at new normal)
  • Months 2–6: Gradual improvement to 3× baseline possible with consistent practice

The question isn't "how fast should I read" — it's "how fast do I read now, and what do I want?" Measure the first part. Then start training for the second.
