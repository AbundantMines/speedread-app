Average Reading Speed: How Do You Compare? (By Age, Profession & Education)

Updated March 2026 · 10 min read

You probably think you're a decent reader. Most people do. But here's the thing: most people have never actually measured their reading speed, and when they do, the number is lower than they expected.

The average adult reads at 238 words per minute. That's not slow — it's normal. But "normal" and "optimal" are very different things. This article breaks down reading speeds by every meaningful category, explains why most people read slower than their brains allow, and shows you exactly where you stand.

Average Reading Speed by Age

Reading speed develops through childhood and adolescence, peaks in early adulthood, and holds relatively steady through middle age before declining slightly in later years. Here are the research-backed averages:

Age Group Average WPM Notes
1st–2nd Grade (6–7) 80–100 Still decoding words; highly variable
3rd–4th Grade (8–9) 100–140 Transitioning from "learning to read" to "reading to learn"
5th–6th Grade (10–11) 140–180 Silent reading becomes dominant
Middle School (12–14) 180–220 Approaching adult baseline
High School (15–18) 200–250 Near adult average
Adults (18–65) 238 The widely cited average (Brysbaert, 2019)
College Students 280–300 Higher due to daily practice with dense material
Seniors (65+) 200–220 Slight decline, mainly from processing speed

The 238 WPM figure comes from a 2019 meta-analysis by Marc Brysbaert that reviewed 190 studies involving over 18,000 participants. It's the most robust estimate we have. Previous commonly cited numbers (250–300 WPM) were based on smaller, less rigorous samples.

Notice that college students read significantly faster than the general adult average. This isn't because they're smarter — it's because they read more. Volume builds speed. The same way a pianist who practices daily plays faster than one who plays monthly.

Average Reading Speed by Profession

Your profession shapes your reading speed more than most people realize. Professionals who read as a core job function consistently outperform the general average.

Profession Estimated WPM Why
General population 238 Baseline
Teachers 260–300 Daily reading of student work and curricula
Journalists 280–320 Deadline-driven consumption of sources
Software Engineers 250–300 Mix of prose and code (code reads slower)
Lawyers 300–350 Massive document review volumes
Doctors / Researchers 280–330 Dense medical literature, trained for precision
Executives / CEOs 300–400 High-volume briefings, reports, and memos
Professional Editors 350–450 Read all day, every day — plus trained eye
Trained Speed Readers 400–700 Deliberate technique + practice

CEOs are famously fast readers. Warren Buffett claims to read 500 pages a day. Bill Gates reads about 50 books a year. Elon Musk reportedly read the entire Encyclopedia Britannica as a child. Whether these are natural gifts or developed skills (probably both), the pattern is clear: high-performers read fast, and reading fast enables high performance. The causation runs both ways.

Average Reading Speed by Material Type

Not all reading is created equal. The same person reads at vastly different speeds depending on what they're reading:

Material Typical WPM
Social media / texts 250–350
Fiction / light nonfiction 230–280
News articles 220–270
Business reports 200–250
Academic papers 150–200
Legal / technical docs 130–180
Foreign language (intermediate) 100–150

This matters because when you test your reading speed, the material matters enormously. A 300 WPM score on a blog post and a 300 WPM score on a legal contract represent very different levels of reading ability.

World Records: How Fast Can Humans Actually Read?

The verified world speed reading records give us an upper bound on human reading performance:

  • Anne Jones (UK): 4,251 WPM — Six-time World Speed Reading Champion. Jones read a Harry Potter book (which she hadn't seen before) at this rate and answered comprehension questions correctly. She's the most credible speed reading champion because her results have been independently verified multiple times.
  • Howard Berg: Claims 25,000 WPM, listed in early Guinness editions. However, these speeds have been disputed by reading researchers, and the Guinness category was later removed due to difficulties in standardizing comprehension verification.
  • Kim Peek: The real "Rain Man" could reportedly read two pages simultaneously (one with each eye) and retained virtually everything. This was a neurological anomaly related to his lack of a corpus callosum, not a trainable skill.

Anne Jones's verified 4,251 WPM is roughly 18x the average reading speed. But even Jones doesn't claim perfect comprehension at that rate — she reports about 67% retention, which drops on complex material. The point isn't to match her but to recognize that the average 238 WPM is nowhere near the biological ceiling.

Why Most People Read Slower Than They Could

If the brain can process text at 400+ WPM, why does the average person top out at 238? Several trainable bottlenecks are to blame.

The Subvocalization Problem

This is the big one. Subvocalization means silently pronouncing words in your head as you read them. Nearly everyone does it, and it caps your speed at roughly the pace of speech: 150–200 WPM.

Think about it — when you read the word "elephant," do you hear it in your head? That mental pronunciation takes time. Multiply it by every word in a sentence, and you've created an artificial speed limit based on your speaking pace, not your comprehension pace.

Your visual system can process words far faster than your auditory system can "hear" them. Reducing subvocalization — not eliminating it entirely, but reducing it to key words — is the single highest-leverage speed reading technique that exists.

Regression (Re-Reading)

Eye-tracking studies show that readers unconsciously move their eyes backward 10–15% of the time. You re-read words and phrases without even knowing it. On a page of 250 words, you might actually be reading 280–290 words of eye movement. That's a 15% speed penalty baked into a bad habit.

Regression is partly a focus problem and partly a confidence problem. Your brain goes back because it's not sure it processed something correctly. A pacer (finger, cursor, or RSVP display) that forces forward movement eliminates this waste immediately.

Narrow Eye Span

Untrained readers fixate on single words. Their effective visual span is 4–6 characters. But the human eye can process 15–20 characters per fixation when trained — enough to take in 2–4 words at once. This means an untrained reader makes 10–12 fixations per line where a trained reader makes 4–5.

Fewer fixations per line = fewer saccadic movements = faster reading. It's pure efficiency.

Lack of Practice at Speed

Most people settled into their reading speed somewhere in high school and never pushed it again. They read at the pace that feels comfortable, which is not the same as the pace their brain can handle. It's like someone who learned to type at 40 WPM in high school and never bothered to improve — they could type twice as fast with a few weeks of deliberate practice, but they never do.

How to Test Your Reading Speed

A proper reading speed test needs three things:

  1. Standardized text: The passage should be grade-level appropriate for adults (roughly 8th–10th grade reading level), on a topic you're not already an expert in, and long enough to get a stable measurement (at least 300 words).
  2. Timed reading: A precise timer that starts when you begin and stops when you finish.
  3. Comprehension check: Speed without comprehension is meaningless. A good test asks 5–10 questions about the content to verify you actually processed what you read.

Your effective reading speed is: WPM × comprehension percentage. If you read at 400 WPM with 50% comprehension, your effective speed is 200 WPM — the same as someone reading at 250 WPM with 80% comprehension. Effective speed is what matters.

Reading Speed vs. Listening Speed: How They Compare

With the rise of audiobooks and text-to-speech, it's worth comparing reading to listening:

  • Normal audiobook speed: 150–160 WPM (standard narration pace)
  • 1.5x audiobook speed: 225–240 WPM (popular among regular listeners)
  • 2x audiobook speed: 300–320 WPM (maximum for most people's comprehension)
  • Average reading speed: 238 WPM
  • Trained reading speed: 400–600 WPM

Even at 2x speed, audiobooks can't match trained reading speed. And there's a neurological reason: visual word processing and auditory word processing use different pathways. The visual pathway has higher bandwidth — you can process written words faster than spoken ones because you control the pace, your eye can jump ahead to grab context, and you can process word shapes in parallel.

Audiobooks are excellent for multitasking (driving, exercise, chores). But for pure information intake speed, reading wins — especially trained reading.
